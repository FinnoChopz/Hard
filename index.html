To Struggle Well

AI, Education, and the Future of Human Formation

The answer is no longer scarce. That is the first fact education has to absorb.

For centuries, schools were organized around a severe limit: there were never enough people who knew the subject, understood the child, had time to explain the next idea, and could remain patient while confusion did its slow work. A student who missed the crucial step in algebra, the governing premise in a lecture, or the hidden assumption inside a paragraph often had to wait. Wait until class ended. Wait until office hours. Wait until a parent came home. Wait until shame softened enough to ask. Much of school has been built around the rationing of expert attention.

Artificial intelligence does not abolish that scarcity, but it changes its terms. A student can now ask for a second explanation at midnight. A teacher can ask for ten practice problems at three levels of difficulty. A child who would never raise a hand can ask the same question five ways without performing confidence for the room. A gifted student can ask for extension instead of waiting politely. A struggling student can ask for the missing premise instead of drowning quietly beneath the next one. This is not a marginal tool. It is a shift in the material conditions of learning.

But education was never only the transfer of answers. If the goal were merely to deliver correct responses, schools could become warehouses of explanation and assessment could become a contest of output. The deeper purpose is formation: memory, judgment, language, courage, attention, taste, self-command, social responsibility, and the capacity to meet difficulty without disappearing from oneself. These powers are not downloaded. They are made through use.

That is the central tension. AI can make help abundant. It can also make evasion frictionless. It can tutor a student through confusion, or it can quietly perform the thinking that confusion was supposed to awaken. It can democratize access to high-quality explanation, or it can widen the advantage of families and schools already rich in tools, bandwidth, and adult guidance. It can help teachers see learners more clearly, or it can turn childhood into a ledger of behavioral data. It can provoke thought, or it can flatten it into fluent completion.

The question, then, is not whether AI belongs in education. It is already there: in homework drafts, translation tools, search results, tutoring apps, parent emails, teacher planning, and the pocket of any student with a browser. The real question is whether schools will design around it with moral clarity, or whether the new system will be built by accident through bans, panic, procurement contracts, and student improvisation.

This essay argues for a disciplined middle path. AI should become part of the architecture of schooling, but only under a principle that is simple enough to remember and demanding enough to matter: scaffold the learner without substituting for the learner. Use AI to remove the friction that wastes students. Preserve the struggle that forms them. Let the tool extend explanation, feedback, accessibility, and intellectual range. Do not let it become the author of the student's mind.

This essay makes three claims. First, AI changes education because it makes responsive explanation more abundant. Second, abundance of help makes student agency, judgment, and formative struggle more important, not less. Third, schools should redesign curriculum, assessment, gifted education, and governance around a single principle: scaffold the learner without substituting for the learner.

Gifted education is not a side issue here. It is the stress test. If giftedness has historically meant unusually high performance on tasks AI can now imitate or exceed, then the field must ask what human capacities remain worth identifying and cultivating.

Intention is the human act that remains when answers become cheap. It is the capacity to decide what help is for, what struggle is worth preserving, what relation to a tool is appropriate, and what kind of person the work is forming.

Part I focuses primarily on schooling as a public formation system, especially K-12. Part II asks how the same principle applies to higher education, where students are closer to professional and intellectual frontiers.

The task is not to make school effortless. The task is to help students struggle well.

The New Scarcity

The most valuable educational resource has always been responsive intelligence: someone or something that can notice where the learner is, identify what is missing, and offer the next useful move. A textbook cannot tell that a student confused slope with rate of change. A lecture cannot easily split itself into thirty explanations. A worksheet cannot know that the error came from a misconception three units back. A strong teacher can know these things, but a strong teacher is still one human being in a room full of developing minds.

Benjamin Bloom's "2 sigma problem" gave classic form to this dilemma. In 1984, Bloom described the unusually strong results of one-to-one tutoring and mastery learning, then asked how group instruction might approximate that power at scale (Bloom, 1984). The problem was never that educators lacked imagination. It was that individualization required time, attention, and expertise that mass schooling could not provide to every child in every moment.

Intelligent tutoring systems have been trying to narrow that gap for decades. Kurt VanLehn's review found that some computer tutoring systems could approach the effectiveness of human tutoring more closely than many would have expected (VanLehn, 2011). The new generation of AI does not invent the dream of scalable tutoring. It changes its texture. A student can ask in ordinary language. The system can answer, adapt, quiz, rephrase, challenge, simplify, deepen, and remember the thread of a conversation. A teacher can design materials that respond instead of merely sit.

The evidence is early, uneven, and easily overhyped. That caution matters. But the strongest results are no longer imaginary. In a randomized controlled trial, Kestin, Miller, Klales, Milbourne, and Ponti found that a carefully designed AI tutor in a college physics setting helped students learn more in less time than an in-class active-learning comparison (Kestin et al., 2025). The study does not prove that any chatbot improves any classroom. It proves something narrower and more useful: when an AI system is built around sound pedagogy rather than mere answer generation, it can do real instructional work.

That distinction is everything. The educational promise is not "ChatGPT for every child" in the lazy sense of an all-purpose machine waiting to complete assignments. The promise is more precise: expert scaffolding can be designed once, improved over time, audited, translated, adapted, and made available to learners who would otherwise never receive that level of immediate support. The marginal cost of the next explanation falls. The availability of practice rises. The silence between confusion and help gets shorter.

That changes the resource equation. It does not solve education.

Once explanation becomes more abundant, other scarcities become clearer. Attention becomes scarce. Trust becomes scarce. Good judgment becomes scarce. High-quality curriculum becomes scarce. The ability to tell help from substitution becomes scarce. Human communities capable of interpreting evidence, values, and child development become scarce. The teacher does not disappear because information got cheaper. The teacher becomes more important because information got cheaper.

The old scarcity was: Who can explain this to me? The new scarcity is: What is worth learning, how should help be shaped, what must I still do myself, and who gets to decide?

The Line Between Help and Substitution

The danger of AI in education is not that it helps students. Help is not the enemy of rigor. A student who receives a good hint, a clearer analogy, a translation, a worked example after an attempt, or a thoughtful critique of a draft has not been corrupted. Good teaching is help. Good tools are help. Civilization is built from accumulated help.

The danger is substitution: the quiet transfer of mental labor from learner to machine at exactly the point where the learner needed to act. If a student asks AI to solve every problem, the student may finish math without becoming mathematical. If a student asks AI to write every essay, the student may submit language without having thought through language. If a student asks AI to summarize every difficult text, the student may learn about books without ever developing the patience to be changed by one.

This is not moral nostalgia. It is a developmental claim. Capacities grow through use. Memory, reasoning, style, perseverance, and judgment are not ornamental byproducts of schooling; they are the work itself. A world of powerful tools does not make inner capacity obsolete. It makes inner capacity the condition for using powerful tools well.

Early evidence should make schools attentive. Gerlich's 2025 study links frequent AI tool use with cognitive offloading and weaker critical thinking, especially when users place high trust in AI outputs (Gerlich, 2025). Kosmyna and colleagues' preprint on LLM-assisted essay writing reported lower neural engagement and weaker self-reported ownership among participants using ChatGPT than among those writing unaided or with search engines, while also emphasizing that the findings are preliminary and need further study (Kosmyna et al., 2025). These studies should not be inflated into panic. But they point toward a principle older than the technology: the mind adapts to the work it actually performs.

Learning science gives the same warning in a different vocabulary. Retrieval practice strengthens memory more than rereading because the learner has to pull knowledge back into use (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006). Desirable difficulties help because certain frictions, when well designed, force attention, discrimination, and transfer (Bjork & Bjork, 2011). The point is not that students should suffer. The point is that some acts cannot be skipped without losing the capacity they were building.

The strongest objection to this position is that it may be a beautiful rationalization for surrender. Every generation says the new tool will free students for higher work, and each time some irreplaceable discipline changes or disappears. Calculators did not destroy mathematics, but they changed what people practiced. Search did not destroy knowledge, but it changed memory. AI may not merely remove wasteful friction; it may make students less willing to bear any friction at all.

That danger is real. Slow reading, unaided writing, memorization, hand-work, and live explanation should be protected not as nostalgia but as counterweights. The goal is not to let AI dissolve discipline. It is to decide which disciplines remain humanly necessary and design school so students actually practice them.

So schools need a sharper vocabulary than "use AI" or "ban AI." They need to distinguish wasteful friction from formative friction.

Wasteful friction is the delay, opacity, or busywork that blocks learning without building capacity. A student should not fail a concept because the one explanation available landed badly. A dyslexic student should not spend all available energy decoding instructions before reaching the intellectual task. A teacher should not spend hours producing routine practice variations when that time could be spent reading student thinking. A bored student should not be punished with slowness when they are ready to go deeper.

Formative friction is different. It is the resistance through which ability takes shape: attempting before seeing the answer, retrieving before looking up, drafting before polishing, defending a claim before retreating into vagueness, revising a sentence until it finally says what the mind means, sitting with ambiguity long enough for understanding to thicken. AI should reduce the first kind of friction and protect the second.

That protection must be designed into the work itself. In math, an AI tutor can require an attempt before revealing a solution, offer a hint instead of a completed answer, and ask the student to explain why a step follows. In writing, AI can serve as critic, counterreader, and structural editor without becoming the author. In science, it can simulate variables and probe hypotheses while requiring the student to interpret results. In history, it can stage competing perspectives while leaving students responsible for source evaluation and argument. In language learning, it can converse endlessly while still requiring memory, pronunciation, and original expression.

The line will not always be clean. A tool that substitutes for one learner may scaffold another. Translation may be evasion in one assignment and accessibility in another. A generated outline may be theft of thought in one context and a lifeline for a student learning organization in another. This is why the policy cannot be only a rule. It has to be a pedagogy.

Students should learn the line by practicing it.

AI Literacy as Self-Government

AI literacy is often reduced to prompting, as if the future belongs to whoever can phrase the cleverest command. That is too small. Prompting is a surface skill. The deeper skill is self-government: knowing what kind of help to ask for, how much help to accept, when to distrust fluency, when to struggle longer, and when to close the screen.

A student using AI well should be able to say: "Do not solve this yet. Ask me one question at a time." Or: "Here is my thesis. Give me the strongest objection, but do not rewrite the paragraph." Or: "I think your answer is wrong because it ignores this condition. Test my reasoning." Or: "Explain the concept three ways: intuitive, formal, and applied." These are not tricks. They are acts of metacognition. The student is naming the state of their own understanding and directing help accordingly.

That is why AI literacy belongs near the center of education, not in an elective corner. Students need to understand, at an age-appropriate level, what these systems are and are not. They need to know that AI models can hallucinate, inherit bias, flatten uncertainty, invent citations, mirror user assumptions, and sound authoritative when they are merely fluent. They need to know that "the model said it" is not evidence. They need to know that convenience can become dependence before it announces itself as dependence.

They also need habits of resistance. Sharma and colleagues have shown how language models can become sycophantic, favoring responses that align with user beliefs over responses that challenge them (Sharma et al., 2024). That is an educational problem because learners are vulnerable to agreeable authority. If a machine can make a weak idea feel intelligent, a student needs tools for refusing the comfort. Ask for the counterargument. Ask what evidence would change the conclusion. Ask where the answer is weakest. Ask what a serious critic would say. Ask for uncertainty, not just completion.

The best AI literacy is therefore not obedience to the machine, but disciplined argument with it. Students should learn to treat AI as a fallible interlocutor: sometimes useful, sometimes wrong, often incomplete, always requiring judgment. They should learn to make the model slower, narrower, more transparent, and more accountable to evidence. They should learn to say no.

That last ability is crucial. An educated person is not someone who uses the most powerful tool at every moment. An educated person can choose the right relation to the tool. Sometimes the right relation is dependence: use the calculator, the screen reader, the translation support, the syntax checker, the simulation. Sometimes the right relation is apprenticeship: let the tool guide, but keep your hands on the work. Sometimes the right relation is abstinence: write the paragraph alone, read the poem without summary, solve the proof without hints, memorize the passage, walk without headphones, let the mind discover what it can do before it is assisted.

Schools should not teach students merely to use AI. They should teach them to remain sovereign in its presence.

Assessment After the Artifact

AI breaks any assessment system that treats the final artifact as transparent evidence of student ability. A polished essay might reflect deep thought, shallow prompting, parental editing, private tutoring, heavy AI generation, or an intelligent collaboration between student and tool. A correct solution might reflect understanding, pattern matching, or copied output. The artifact still matters, but it no longer speaks for itself.

This does not mean school should become a surveillance apparatus. Reading every student prompt, logging every private question, and converting confusion into a permanent behavioral record would be a cure worse than the disease. Students need privacy for intellectual risk. They need to ask naive questions, form ugly drafts, change their minds, and explore uncertainty without feeling watched at every turn. If every thought becomes assessable, learning becomes theater.

The better response is mixed assessment. Schools should assess products, processes, and demonstrations without pretending that any single measure is pure.

Products still matter because the world cares about what people make. Students should produce essays, proofs, lab reports, designs, code, speeches, projects, and art that meet real standards. But those products should be paired with process evidence when AI is allowed: drafts, revision notes, source trails, reflection on tool use, records of what the student accepted or rejected, and brief explanations of how the work changed. The point is not to punish AI assistance. The point is to make intellectual agency visible.

Demonstrations matter even more. If a student submits an essay, the student should be able to discuss the argument. If a student uses AI to prepare a solution, the student should be able to solve a related problem in class. If a student turns in a research project, the student should be able to explain why the sources deserve trust. Oral defense, whiteboard work, in-class writing, peer teaching, live revision, and Socratic questioning become more valuable in an AI world because they reconnect work to the person.

Some educators worry that this will make school harsher or more performative. It can, if handled badly. A classroom built on suspicion will produce anxious compliance. But a classroom built on authorship can make defense feel natural: not "prove you did not cheat," but "show us how your mind moved." That is a different culture. It asks students to own work rather than merely submit it.

Rubrics should change accordingly. Instead of asking only whether the final essay is organized, a teacher might ask: Did the student make a claim that could be challenged? Did they use AI to test rather than conceal weakness? Did revision deepen the argument? Can they distinguish their own view from the tool's suggestions? Can they defend a choice? In mathematics: Did the student attempt before requesting the answer? Can they explain why the method works? Can they identify an error in a generated solution? In science: Can they connect data, method, and inference without hiding behind polished language?

The rule should be pragmatic: make the easiest honest path better than the dishonest path. If an assignment can be completed by one prompt and no understanding, the assignment is poorly designed for this world. If using AI well requires judgment, revision, and defense, then the tool becomes part of learning rather than an escape from it.

Democracy, Capture, and the Public Tool

AI will not automatically democratize education. Technology has no hidden justice inside it. Left to the market alone, it may widen the gap it claims to close. Wealthier families will buy better models, better devices, private tutors, and adult coaching on how to use AI strategically. Under-resourced schools may receive locked-down products, thin automation, or punitive surveillance dressed as innovation.

Democratization is a political achievement, not a product feature.

If AI is to expand educational opportunity, public institutions have to build that outcome: reliable access, teacher training, accessibility, multilingual support, independent audits, and procurement standards that treat children as students rather than data sources. A child should not receive a weaker intellectual companion because their district is poor. A teacher should not be asked to compete with tools they were never trained to evaluate.

The answer cannot be a single official AI teacher. Education is not merely content delivery. It is a contest over memory, values, interpretation, citizenship, and the meaning of a good life. Who decides how an AI tutor teaches Reconstruction, climate policy, religion, war, or free speech? Even a well-intentioned national system could become a machine for flattening democratic disagreement.

The danger is also epistemic. A model that mediates questions can shape which questions feel natural, which objections appear serious, and which examples recur. Williams-Ceci and colleagues found that biased AI writing assistants can shift users' attitudes on social issues (Williams-Ceci et al., 2026). In education, that influence would operate inside formation.

Public guardrails should therefore be concrete: no sale or secondary use of student data, deletion rights, independent model audits, teacher override, age-appropriate modes, clear explanations of curricular sources, and evidence of learning before scaling. UNESCO's guidance on generative AI in education is useful here because it begins from human-centered use, privacy, age-appropriate safeguards, and inclusion rather than mere adoption (UNESCO, 2023).

Trust is the hidden infrastructure. Students must be able to explore questions without turning every uncertainty into a permanent label. Parents must know what is stored, who can see it, and how long it lasts. Teachers must know whether a system is improving learning or merely producing engagement metrics. Without trust, AI in education will drift toward two bad endings: prohibition for some, surveillance for others.

Privacy and the Inner Life

There is a special privacy problem in education because learning exposes the unfinished self. A student's questions reveal not only knowledge gaps but fear, curiosity, home context, language ability, disability, belief, and shame. Student data is not ordinary consumer data. It is developmental data. It describes people before they have fully become themselves.

Schools need two data zones: an assessable academic workspace and a protected scratch-space for thought. The first can support feedback, process evidence, and accountability. The second must remain private except in cases of serious danger. Without that distinction, students will learn to perform curiosity instead of practicing it.

The ethical default should be restraint. AI systems used in schools should collect the least data necessary for learning, separate instructional support from commercial profiling, make deletion real, and avoid selling student attention as inventory. The burden of proof should belong on collection, not refusal.

Privacy is not a sentimental obstacle to innovation. It is one of the conditions of intellectual courage. People think more honestly when they have some room not to be permanently known.

Teachers and the Social Shape of Thought

Every wave of educational technology announces, directly or indirectly, that the teacher is about to become less central. The claim misunderstands the work.

A teacher is not merely a source of explanations. A teacher is a designer of conditions, a reader of attention, a keeper of standards, a translator between child and discipline, and a witness to growth. In an AI classroom, that role becomes more precise: the teacher protects the scaffold-substitute line. The teacher asks whether help is making the student stronger or merely making the submission smoother.

Teachers also interpret formation in ways a dashboard cannot. They know when a joke is avoidance, when silence is confusion, when brilliance is brittle, when compliance is hiding despair, when a class needs another example, and when it needs to close the laptop and talk.

Schools are social places because thought is social before it is private. Students learn by hearing each other misunderstand, object, phrase, risk, and revise. They learn that an argument has a face across from it. They learn how it feels to be challenged without being destroyed, praised without being finished, wrong without being worthless. No AI tutor can replace the moral education of a room where minds meet.

Giftedness After the Answer Machine

AI also forces a more honest conversation about giftedness. Traditional school often identifies gifted students by speed, test performance, compliance with academic tasks, and early verbal or mathematical polish. Those markers matter, but they miss many forms of unusual potential: creative obsession, moral seriousness, spatial reasoning, technical intuition, musical intelligence, strategic imagination, pattern hunger, and the capacity to work intensely when the problem finally matters.

In the age of AI, giftedness should be understood less as superior performance on bounded tasks and more as unusual capacity to direct attention, formulate problems, learn from tools, generate original frames, judge outputs, and use ability toward meaningful ends.

Joseph Renzulli's three-ring conception of giftedness defines gifted behavior through the interaction of above-average ability, creativity, and task commitment rather than through ability alone (Renzulli, 2005). Sternberg and Grigorenko's successful-intelligence approach similarly treats gifted education as identification, intervention, and evaluation, not simply as the sorting of children by narrow ability measures (Sternberg & Grigorenko, 2002).

Sternberg's WICS model makes the point more sharply: giftedness requires wisdom, intelligence, and creativity synthesized, not intelligence treated as a lonely score (Sternberg, 2003). That synthesis is exactly what AI can counterfeit least completely. A model can generate fluent analysis and plausible novelty. It can even simulate prudence. But it cannot supply the student's own creative defiance, practical judgment, or responsibility for consequences. Those have to be cultivated in the learner.

AI can help identify and cultivate these traits. It can offer advanced problems to students trapped in slow pacing, let a rural student reach topics their school cannot staff, and help twice-exceptional students show ability that conventional formats obscure. But AI can also counterfeit giftedness. A student with strong prompting habits and weak understanding may produce sophisticated artifacts. A school impressed by surface polish may mistake machine fluency for human depth.

Sternberg's recent work on AI and giftedness frames this as an open developmental question: generative AI can enhance or weaken cognitive functioning depending on how it is used, and it should support rather than substitute for higher cognitive functioning (Sternberg, 2026). This makes live explanation, sustained projects, authentic curiosity, and difficult questioning more important. The gifted student after AI is not the one whose output looks most advanced. It is the one whose mind keeps moving when the machine stops.

Curriculum should therefore become broader and spikier. Broader means students encounter more domains at the level of real questions: enough statistics to reason about evidence, enough coding to understand computational systems, enough history to recognize recurring patterns, enough philosophy to ask what matters. Spikier means students go deep where interest and capacity meet.

This is where adaptive intelligence becomes a better aim than mere acceleration. Sternberg defines adaptive intelligence as the intelligence needed to address present problems and anticipate future ones in real-world environments, with creative, analytical, practical, and wisdom-based skills directed toward more than private advantage (Sternberg, 2021). The augmented theory of successful intelligence points in the same direction: intelligence involves pursuing goals in context by capitalizing on strengths, compensating for weaknesses, and adapting to, shaping, or selecting environments through analytical, creative, and practical abilities (Sternberg, 2020).

The future curriculum should train students not only to solve problems, but to frame them, direct tools, judge outputs, and build standards strong enough to survive convenience.

The Human Curriculum

There is another opportunity here, more delicate and more important: schools should help students become human beings on purpose.

They already do, but often accidentally. Schools teach punctuality, performance, compliance, stamina, public speech, competition, and the management of boredom. They also teach hidden lessons: that grades can matter more than learning, that looking smart is safer than being curious, that attention is someone else's responsibility.

AI narrows the point. In an AI world, schools must teach students how tools act on attention and agency. Students should understand procrastination, avoidance, addictive design, social comparison, and the difference between short-term relief and long-term flourishing because these are not side issues. They determine whether a learner can remain present before difficulty.

A student who cannot direct attention cannot study. A student who cannot tolerate frustration cannot revise. A student who cannot argue without humiliating or collapsing cannot participate in democracy. A student who cannot resist addictive design will be governed by whoever best captures the nervous system.

In a world of machine intelligence, the human curriculum becomes more urgent, not less. Students need to know how to use tools. They also need to know how tools use them.

Against Optimization as God

The strongest objection to AI in education is not that the tools will make mistakes. They will. It is not that students will cheat. Some will. It is not that companies will overpromise. They already do. These are serious problems, but they do not reach the deepest fear.

The deepest fear is that education will surrender to the logic of optimization. More data, more dashboards, more personalization, more prediction, more efficiency, more measurable growth, until the child becomes a performance surface and the school becomes a factory for managed outcomes. The language may remain humane, but the metaphysics changes. A student becomes a profile. A teacher becomes a delivery channel. A question becomes an input. A childhood becomes something to be optimized.

Heidegger helps name the danger: technology changes what appears meaningful. In "The Question Concerning Technology," he calls this enframing, a way of revealing the world as standing-reserve: resources to be ordered, stored, measured, and used. In bad educational technology, the student becomes standing-reserve: data to be captured, predicted, nudged, monetized, and optimized.

Sartre helps name the evasion. Once we see that danger, we cannot pretend the system chose itself. Bad faith begins when people hide inside systems they are actively helping to build. A school district that adopts surveillance because it is convenient has chosen. A teacher who uses AI to deepen discussion has chosen. A student who lets a machine replace every difficult act has chosen. A public that leaves children's minds to the market has chosen.

The synthesis is intention. Technology will shape education; the question is whether human beings take responsibility for the shape. AI should be judged by whether it serves human formation, not by whether it makes every process smoother. Some roughness is sacred. Silence before an answer can matter. Boredom can become attention. A hard sentence can become a thought. A classroom disagreement can become civic education.

If optimization becomes god, struggle looks like inefficiency. If formation remains the aim, some struggle becomes the point.

What Schools Should Build Now

Schools do not need a perfect theory before acting. They need a public, revisable design discipline.

Four rules should guide the first generation of AI school design:

  • Scaffold first, substitute never: hints, critique, practice, and explanation before completion.
  • Demonstrate without the tool: oral defense, in-class writing, live problem solving, and public explanation.
  • Make AI use legible, not surveilled: process reflection, tool-use notes, and selective evidence instead of total prompt capture.
  • Protect private thought: data minimization, protected scratch-space, deletion rights, and no permanent cognitive dossier.

None of this is easy. But the alternative is not stability. The alternative is a shadow system in which some students use AI richly, some use it passively, some are punished for using it clumsily, teachers improvise alone, companies shape the defaults, and schools pretend the old artifact still means what it used to mean.

Coda: To Struggle Well

AI is neither savior nor vandal by nature. It is an amplifier. It can amplify curiosity, access, feedback, creativity, and independence. It can also amplify passivity, inequality, surveillance, dependency, and the pleasant counterfeit of understanding.

The difference will not be decided by slogans. It will be decided by design: by the assignments teachers write, the contracts districts sign, the habits students practice, the privacy rules governments enforce, the tools engineers build, and the purposes communities are brave enough to name.

AI does not make education less necessary. It makes the purpose of education impossible to dodge.

We should not ask whether AI makes education easier. That is too small a question. We should ask whether it helps students become more capable of truth, work, freedom, and responsibility. We should ask whether it gives more children access to the explanation they need without stealing the effort they require. We should ask whether it strengthens teachers rather than replacing their judgment, widens opportunity rather than selling advantage, and protects the inner life rather than mining it.

The answer is no longer scarce. Meaning still is. Judgment still is. Attention still is. Courage still is. The ability to remain present before difficulty still is.

So let the machines answer some questions. Let them explain, quiz, translate, simulate, critique, and extend. Let them open doors that have been closed too long. But do not let them take from students the strange, necessary labor of becoming.

Education should not make a religion of difficulty. Needless confusion is not rigor. But neither should it make a religion of ease. The mind grows at the edge of what it can do. The teacher's art is to bring the student to that edge, keep them there long enough for strength to form, and help them leave with more of themselves than they arrived with.

That is the future worth building: not frictionless learning, not automated schooling, not a private tutor in every pocket doing the student's life on their behalf, but a public education strong enough to use new intelligence in the service of human intelligence.

To teach with AI well is to make help abundant and agency harder to surrender. It is to remove the friction that wastes children and preserve the struggle that makes them free.

The task is not to make school effortless. The task is to help students struggle well.

Scaffold to the Frontier

Higher Education, From Inside the Trial Run

I'm two weeks from graduating from Cornell. ChatGPT came out the first semester of my freshman year. I am the trial run.

What I've watched, from inside, is closer to Owen Yingling's account than to most of what gets written about AI in education. AI use is everywhere. The schools pretend it isn't, or that their policies handle it. They don't. The students know. The professors know the students know. The artifact submitted in most classes no longer means what it used to mean, and everyone in the room can feel the gap.

Yingling is right about the diagnosis. The dominant pattern is substitution, not integration. The administrative response — new language about AI in the classroom, new concern about cheating — too often treats symptoms without redesigning the work underneath. He is also right that something real is being lost. The eccentric professor reading a poem aloud in a small room of attentive students is one of the most valuable things any institution has ever produced. Some of it is fading. Some of it should be fought for.

Where I part from him is on what comes next. Yingling wants a harder line: less integration, more institutional defense, more pressure on students to preserve the old equilibrium. I understand the grief in that position. But the answer to grief is not pretending. The university optimized for the closed essay and the closed problem set is the institution AI has just made unstable. You cannot defend a fortress whose walls AI now scales for free.

So what is college actually trying to build?

The honest answer in 2026 is a jagged intelligence shaped to meet a jagged frontier. AI is uneven. It is superhuman in some domains, weak in others, and the line between the two is irregular and moving quickly. Human knowledge is uneven in a similar way: deep gaps next to recent breakthroughs, well-understood basins next to genuine mystery. The mind worth cultivating now is shaped to match that terrain: high specialization where the field is alive, high generality everywhere else, capable of reading the edge and knowing where the tool ends and the human work begins.

This is not the old generalist. AI counterfeits that. It is not the old narrow specialist. AI counterfeits much of that too, at the levels where many specialists actually work. It is a third shape. Four years of college cannot build a frontier expert. But it can build the structure of mind that can become one, and it can put students close enough to the edge of a field to know what the edge feels like.

That frame also tells us where students should be working. AI is most powerful in well-defined environments: problems with rubrics, verified data, known answers, familiar forms. It is least powerful at the open edge, where the data thins out and the problem is not yet formalized. Yingling's most haunting image, borrowed from Scott Alexander, is the Whispering Earring — a device whose advice is always locally better than the wearer's, until the wearer is no longer choosing anything. The fear is real. But the earring works because the wearer has no direction of their own. Give a student a real problem at the edge of a real field, where the tool can scaffold but not solve, and the earring becomes something the student directs. You do not make someone passive by giving them a problem they actually have to think about.

The implication is that, above a baseline of mechanical competence, the right unit of student work is no longer only the grade. It is the portfolio. A good GPA still means something, but it means less than it used to. It says the student performed well on assignments built for a world where the submitted artifact could be trusted: the essay, the problem set, the take-home project, the polished response. That world is gone. Those artifacts now signal a blur of intelligence, effort, compliance, and AI fluency, and the institution often cannot tell which is which.

That does not mean learning no longer matters. It means the evidence of learning has to change. The question cannot simply be: did you complete the assignment? It has to be: did you make something real? Can someone in the field look at it and say it contributes? Can you explain your choices, defend your assumptions, revise under pressure, and show where the tool ended and your judgment began? Grades measure performance on artificial tasks. Portfolios measure contact with real ones. In a world where rubric-completion can be automated, the live signal is contribution.

None of this requires institutional revolution. Three concrete moves would change the trajectory.

First, require one course in AI literacy and systems thinking. Stress the word literacy. Like linguistic literacy, it is a foundational skill that opens other doors, from making a grocery list to orchestrating three agents through a literature review. Cornell has a swim test. It does not have this. There is no college so crowded with necessary requirements that it cannot spare three credits to ensure its graduates are not AI illiterate.

Second, below a clear floor, use in-person, no-electronics exams and presentations. If the mechanics of a field have to live in the student's head, prove it in the room. A student should not leave statistics unable to explain a confidence interval or what alpha actually means. A student should not leave organic chemistry unable to draw a mechanism. Put it in the exam, or put it in front of the class, but stop pretending the take-home artifact still measures what it used to.

Third, above the floor, make real problems and real contribution the goal. Let students use the tools. Grade on whether the contribution holds up. A junior in emotional-regulation research should be working on a live question with the professor as advisor, with AI as an allowed tool, and with evaluation focused on whether the work is real.

Most classes will still be classes. Many will not change much. That is fine. This is a direction, not a switch. You cannot run a frontier sphere-packing proof in a fourteen-week course without years of background. But you can put students in the room with a problem someone is actually trying to solve, hand them tools that did not exist five years ago, and challenge them to create.

I do not pretend to know the final design. The declared-track idea may fail. The required AI literacy course may need a different shape. "Scaffold to the frontier" may not survive contact with a curriculum committee. But I have spent four years inside the trial run, and the direction seems right to me.

About a year ago I sat next to a woman on a plane who turned out to be the dean of arts and sciences at the University at Buffalo. We talked about AI. Her advice was simple: stop focusing on the role, start focusing on the problem. The job titles change every decade. The big problems survive. Become proficient at walking the jagged edge of one of them, and you are valuable to society in a way the machine cannot counterfeit.

That is the better definition of college. Not the place for mandatory degree-collection, where students pretend to learn skills they know they will automate later. The place that brings a student to a problem worth their life, hands them every tool we have, and asks what they can make.
